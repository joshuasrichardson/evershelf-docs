# EverShelf — Ideal Customer Profiles

## Segment 1: The Meal Prepper

### Who they are
- **Age:** 25–40
- **Lifestyle:** Busy professionals, parents, or fitness-oriented individuals who batch-cook on weekends
- **Income:** Middle income; value efficiency over luxury
- **Tech comfort:** High — already using apps for recipes, grocery lists, or macro tracking
- **Where they hang out:** r/MealPrepSunday, r/EatCheapAndHealthy, r/fitmeals, YouTube meal prep channels, TikTok cooking content

### Core problem
They plan meals every week but constantly run into the same friction: they don't know exactly what they already have, they buy duplicates, and they waste time hunting for recipes that match their current inventory. Meal planning feels like it *should* be simple, but the gap between "what's in my kitchen" and "what should I make" is surprisingly painful.

### What they want from EverShelf
- See everything in their kitchen at a glance
- Get recipe suggestions that use what they already have
- Generate a full weekly meal plan without starting from scratch
- A grocery list that only includes what they're actually missing

### Feature hooks
- AI meal plan generation
- Recipe suggestions from current inventory
- Photo-based food detection for fast entry
- Expiration alerts so they use items before they go bad

### How to talk to them
"Stop planning meals in the dark. EverShelf knows what's in your kitchen and builds your meal plan around it."

## Segment 2: The Food Waste Fighter

### Who they are
- **Age:** 22–45
- **Lifestyle:** Environmentally conscious, may identify as zero-waste or sustainability-focused
- **Income:** Varies; motivated more by values than budget (though saving money is a bonus)
- **Tech comfort:** Moderate to high
- **Where they hang out:** r/ZeroWaste, r/sustainability, r/Anticonsumption, zero-waste blogs, local buy-nothing groups

### Core problem
They hate throwing away food. They know household food waste is a massive environmental issue and they feel personally guilty every time something expires in the back of their fridge. They've tried spreadsheets, fridge whiteboards, and sheer willpower — but nothing sticks because it requires too much manual effort to maintain.

### What they want from EverShelf
- Effortless tracking of what they have and when it expires
- Proactive alerts before things go bad
- Meal suggestions that prioritize items about to expire
- A way to share surplus food with friends instead of trashing it
- Visibility into their actual waste patterns over time

### Feature hooks
- Expiration date tracking with push notification reminders
- Expiration heatmap (visual urgency)
- AI prioritizes expiring items in meal plans
- Community food sharing with friends
- Disposal tracking (used / donated / composted / thrown away)

### How to talk to them
"The average household throws away $1,500 of food per year. EverShelf tracks what you have, alerts you before it expires, and helps you share what you won't use."

## Segment 3: The Budget-Conscious Home Cook

### Who they are
- **Age:** 20–55
- **Lifestyle:** Cost-conscious families, students, or anyone actively trying to stretch their grocery budget
- **Income:** Lower to middle income; grocery spending is a meaningful line item
- **Tech comfort:** Moderate
- **Where they hang out:** r/EatCheapAndHealthy, r/Frugal, r/budgetfood, r/povertyfinance, coupon/deal communities

### Core problem
They spend more on groceries than they need to because they forget what they have, buy things twice, and let food expire. They want to cook at home more but the planning overhead (what to buy, what to make, what's going bad) makes it stressful. Every wasted item feels like money in the trash.

### What they want from EverShelf
- Know exactly what's in the kitchen so they stop buying duplicates
- Get notified before things expire so nothing goes to waste
- Recipes that use what they have so they don't need to buy more
- A clear picture of what they're running low on vs. what they're stocked up on

### Feature hooks
- Full pantry/fridge/freezer inventory
- Expiration tracking with alerts
- Recipe suggestions from current inventory
- Gap analysis (what you need vs. what you have)

### How to talk to them
"Know what you have. Use what you have. Stop buying what you don't need. EverShelf is a pantry tracker that saves you money without any extra effort."

## Segment 4: The Preparedness-Minded Household

### Who they are
- **Age:** 30–60
- **Lifestyle:** Preppers, homesteaders, rural families, or anyone who maintains a deep pantry for emergencies
- **Income:** Middle to upper-middle; willing to invest in preparedness
- **Tech comfort:** Moderate — will adopt purpose-built tools but skeptical of bloat
- **Where they hang out:** r/preppers, r/homesteading, r/survival, r/TwoXPreppers, survivalist forums, preparedness YouTube

### Core problem
They maintain significant food storage (weeks to months worth) but have no good system to track it. Spreadsheets get stale. They don't know if their storage is nutritionally balanced. They can't quickly answer "how many days could we actually sustain ourselves?" They need rotation management so older items get used first.

### What they want from EverShelf
- Full inventory across multiple storage locations
- Nutritional audit: am I actually prepared, or do I just have 50 cans of beans?
- Gap analysis: what categories am I missing?
- Expiration management for large inventories
- Days-of-preparedness calculation based on household size

### Feature hooks
- Survival Six audit (grains, protein, fats/oils, sugars/salt, vitamin C, water)
- Days of survival calculation by household size and calorie needs
- Diversity score for nutritional balance
- Gap analysis with purchase suggestions
- Multi-location tracking (pantry, basement, garage, etc.)
- Expiration alerts for rotation management

### How to talk to them
"EverShelf audits your food storage against six nutritional categories, calculates your days of preparedness, and tells you exactly where the gaps are."

## Segment 5: The Community Sharer

### Who they are
- **Age:** 25–45
- **Lifestyle:** Social, neighborhood-oriented, may already participate in buy-nothing groups or community gardens
- **Income:** Varies
- **Tech comfort:** High — already using social/community apps
- **Where they hang out:** r/ZeroWaste, r/BuyNothing, Facebook buy-nothing groups, Nextdoor, community garden forums

### Core problem
They regularly have surplus food — from their garden, bulk buying, or simply overestimating what they'll eat — and they want to give it away rather than waste it. Coordinating this through group chats or Facebook posts is clunky. They want a lightweight way to list what they have, see what friends have, and coordinate handoffs.

### What they want from EverShelf
- List surplus items for friends to see
- Request items from friends easily
- In-app chat to coordinate pickups
- A system that feels like sharing, not a marketplace

### Feature hooks
- Community food sharing (list surplus, browse friends' items)
- Item request workflow with automatic chat threads
- Handoff confirmation (items auto-archived after transfer)
- Invite codes for easy friend onboarding

### How to talk to them
"Have extra food? List it. Need something? Request it. EverShelf makes sharing with friends as easy as sending a text."

## Segment Overlap & Priority

Most real users will span 2–3 segments. The most common overlaps:

| Primary Segment | Common Overlap |
|---|---|
| Meal Prepper | + Budget-Conscious Cook |
| Food Waste Fighter | + Community Sharer |
| Budget-Conscious Cook | + Food Waste Fighter |
| Preparedness Household | (tends to be standalone) |

### Recommended priority for early growth:
1. **Meal Prepper** — largest addressable audience, clearest value prop, easiest to demonstrate
2. **Food Waste Fighter** — strong emotional motivation, great word-of-mouth potential
3. **Preparedness Household** — underserved niche with no good competitor, highly engaged community
4. **Budget-Conscious Cook** — large audience but overlaps heavily with segments 1 and 2
5. **Community Sharer** — powerful differentiator but requires network effects (friends need to be on the app too)
